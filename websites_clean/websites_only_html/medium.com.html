We Have Been Seeing a Lot of Collapsed Lungs With COVID-19

It seems that COVID, or the immune response to it, can cause lung destruction

Dr. Hesham A. Hassaballa
May 14, 2020 · 4 min read

It happens all of a sudden. Without warning, the patient’s skin becomes puffy. Sometimes, it is in the neck. Other times, it is on the chest. I have even seen it happen with a patient’s face. When you feel the skin, it feels like “rice crispies.”

The medical term for this is subcutaneous emphysema. It occurs when air escapes from the lungs and enters the soft tissues. Sometimes, air will track in the middle of the chest causing a condition called pneumomediastinum. The worst-case scenario is the outright collapse of one of the lungs, called a pneumothorax.

We have been seeing it a lot more with our COVID patients.

How does air escape from the lungs into the skin?

Normally, air should not be in the skin at all. Normally, all of the air in the lungs should stay in the lungs. Sometimes, however, the lung ruptures and air escapes from the lung into the chest cavity, causing the lung to collapse and cause shortness of breath and low oxygen levels.

This can commonly happen with penetrating trauma to the chest, such as a stab or gunshot wound. Other times, the lungs do not collapse, and the air finds its way out of the chest and into the tissues.

We have been seeing both scenarios with patients suffering from COVID-19.

Many times, this can happen when patients are on the ventilator: the high pressures generated by the machine can cause the lungs to rupture and cause lung collapse.

This is part of the reason why we are trying to avoid placing patients on ventilators unless they absolutely require it. At the same time, I have seen collapsed lungs and air in the soft tissues on a number of COVID patients who are not on ventilators.

This should not happen normally, and I don’t recall seeing this phenomenon before COVID-19.

Why is this happening with COVID-19?

It is hard to know with total certainty why this is happening. Most likely, the lung is getting destroyed by this infection. This destruction may be due to the virus itself, or — just as likely — the body’s immune response to the viral infection.

Our immune fighting cells secrete many enzymes that can destroy healthy lung tissue. These cells don’t care about causing “collateral damage.” All they care is about is eradicating the infection.

For these immune cells, the ends justify the means.

When lung tissue gets destroyed, air can then escape from the lungs into either the chest cavity or the skin and soft tissues. This can happen with anything that generates positive pressure in the chest: cough or sneeze or the CPAP that some patients are wearing to help them provide oxygen to the lungs. when the lung collapses, a tube must be inserted into the chest cavity to drain the air.

This can be dangerous, especially if the air gets trapped in the chest cavity and builds up pressure to the point that it causes shock and heart failure. This is — thank God — pretty rare. And if the air in the soft tissues is massive, little holes need to be made in the skin to let the air escape. This is also, thank God, pretty rare.

Most of the time, the air that gets trapped in the tissues causes an annoyance and will get reabsorbed by the body over time.

So many things have been “novel” with this novel coronavirus infection. Doctors have seen things with this disease we have never seen before in our careers. While the disease itself and its effects on our patients have been quite frightening, the physiology and pathophysiology have been fascinating.

I never knew people could have such low oxygen levels and not be gasping for breath.

I never knew that lungs could collapse even when not on a ventilator.

When this is all over — which, sadly, will not be for a very long time — I know that I will be a much better doctor having learned much from this virus and what it can do to the body.

In that, I pray, is a silver lining to this vast dark cloud.
